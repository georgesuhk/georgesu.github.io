Numerical simulation of KdeV and KP soliton dynamics

Computational physics/mathematical project developing a simulation of soliton solutions formed by the KdeV and KP partial differential equations (PDEs). The PDEs are solved using a range of Runge Kutta (RK) methods and a Finite Difference (FD) discretisation scheme. The stability and validity of the simulations were examined by comparing the simulated behavior to expected theoretical behavior.

Background

The Kortewag-deVries (KdeV) equations

and its two dimensional analog, the KadomtsevPetviashvili (KP) equation:

are two non-linear partial differential equations (PDE) which have been of particular interest in physical research due to the unique properties of their analytical solutions. The solutions resemble solitary waves, and are able to preserves their shape asymptotically during propagation. For these properties, they have been given the name "solitons". Solitons also have the unusual property of preserving their shape and velocity during collisions. Solitons have become crucial in the modelling of phenomena involving non-linearity and dispersion, which are prominent in the areas such as plasma physics and hydrodynamics.

Quick theory

KdeV equation

The KdeV equation is able to exhibit soliton solutions due to the fine balance between the non-linear second term and the dispersive third term. The non-linear term leads to a dependence of the wave speed on its amplitude, which results in the steepening of the wave in the direction of propagation as the peak catches up with the trough. The interplay of non-linearity and dispersion also leads to the nuanced characteristics of soliton collision. Solitons are able to preserve their shape and velocity during a collision as they simply transfer their amplitude and hence velocity to one another, without actually superposing. A corollary of this property is the conservation of the area (integral) of soliton solutions. In actuality, it is proven that the KdeV equation has infinitely many conserved integrals of motion (IoM), which serves as a useful benchmark for checking the stability of numerical simulations. Only specific initial conditions will behave as a soliton, and propagate stably for all time. Other initial conditions will result in the phenomena of wave breaking, where the initial condition breaks down into multiple solitons of lower amplitudes.

KP equation

The KP equation like its one-dimensional counterpart, describes wave-like propagation in the $x$ direction, but now with the additional effect of dispersion along the $y$ direction. The KP equation possesses two types of normal modes - line solitons, which resembles the one-dimensional KdeV soliton extended across a linear direction in two-dimensional space. and lumps, which as the name suggests, resembles a peak that decays to zero in all spatial directions. Less are understood about lumps, but they are expected to also propagate as solitary waves.

Simulation methods

The numerical methods chosen were the RK2, 3, 4 and RKF45 methods. Analytical stability analysis is performed for the methods and the stability regions are as following:


A) From in to out: The stability region on the imaginary plane for RK1-4 methods. B) The spatial step h and time step Δt requirements for stability for RK3 and 4.

The stability analysis was performed using the matrix method. Since the maximum eigenvalue of the Kdev and KP equations line on the imaginary axis, we see that RK1 and 2 methods are unstable for all conditions. Higher order RK methods are stable if the time step and spatial step of discretisation fulfills certain conditions, and the conditions are more relaxed for higher order methods.

The simulation of collision above is ran with a combination of h and Δt that is within the stability region of RK4 but not RK3. During the collision, the first order integral of motion remains very constant, while the deviation grows for higher orders, but even for the third order, the deviation still remains below 0.01%.

Wave breaking (non solition initial condition)

The above animation shows the response of the simulation to a positive sine wave initial condition. When starting in a non-normal mode, both the effects of non-linear steepening and dispersion no longer perfectly counteracts one another. In subsequent time slices, we see the the sine wave steepen in the direction of propagation, while also spreading out due to dispersion. Ultimately the two effects balance each other and the initial profile breaks down into multiple stable soliton solutions.
